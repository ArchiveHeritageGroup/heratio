# What the EU AI Act Means for Galleries, Libraries, Archives and Museums

*By Johan Pieterse, The Archive and Heritage Group | May 2026*

Artificial intelligence has quietly become part of everyday collections work. Handwritten letters are transcribed automatically, names and places are tagged as they are catalogued, faded photographs are enhanced, and visitors increasingly ask a chatbot rather than a reference desk. The European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation 2024/1689) is the first comprehensive law to put rules around all of this, and a fresh round of negotiations this month has just reshaped the timeline. Here is what it means for the GLAM sector, in plain language.

## The Act in one paragraph

The AI Act sorts AI systems by risk. A small number of uses are banned outright (the "unacceptable risk" tier). A defined set of "high-risk" uses carry heavy obligations. A middle tier of "limited risk" uses, such as chatbots and synthetic media, mainly trigger transparency duties. Everything else is "minimal risk" and largely unregulated. Separate rules apply to the large general-purpose AI (GPAI) models, such as the foundation models that power many of the tools we now use.

## The timeline just moved

The Act entered into force on 1 August 2024 and applies in phases:

- **2 February 2025**: the bans on prohibited practices took effect, and a new AI literacy duty began.
- **2 August 2025**: the obligations for general-purpose AI model providers began to apply.
- **2 August 2026**: the European Commission's enforcement powers over GPAI take effect, and the transparency rules (including labelling of AI-generated content) begin.

The headline change this month: under the EU's "Digital Omnibus" simplification package, negotiators reached a provisional agreement on 6 May 2026, confirmed by member states in the Council on 13 May, to **defer the high-risk obligations**. Stand-alone high-risk systems (Annex III) now move to **2 December 2027**, and high-risk AI embedded in regulated products (Annex I) to **2 August 2028**. Formal adoption is expected shortly. The practical takeaway is that institutions have more breathing room on the heaviest obligations, but the bans, the literacy duty and the transparency rules are here now or arriving in 2026.

## Why most heritage AI is not "high-risk" (but is not unregulated either)

This is the reassuring part. The everyday AI in a GLAM setting (handwritten text recognition, named-entity recognition, automated metadata suggestions, condition assessment, search relevance, machine translation of finding aids) generally falls into the minimal or limited-risk tiers. None of it is on the banned list, and most of it is not high-risk.

That does not mean there is nothing to do. Three duties reach almost every institution that deploys AI:

1. **AI literacy (already in force).** Organisations that use AI must ensure the people operating it have a sufficient understanding of what it does and its limits. For a library or archive, that means your cataloguers and reference staff should know, at a working level, what your transcription or tagging tools can and cannot be trusted to do.

2. **Transparency for AI-generated content (from August 2026).** Where content is generated or materially altered by AI (a synthetic image, an AI-written description, a machine translation, a restored or upscaled photograph), it should be disclosed and, where applicable, machine-readable as AI-generated. For collections, the honest answer to "did a human or a model write this metadata?" is becoming a labelling requirement, not just good manners.

3. **Chatbot disclosure (from August 2026).** If your catalogue or website offers an AI assistant, users must be told they are interacting with a machine, not a person.

## Two things to watch more carefully

A couple of uses sit closer to the regulated edge and deserve a second look:

- **Facial recognition and biometrics.** The untargeted scraping of facial images to build or expand facial-recognition databases is prohibited, and emotion recognition is restricted in some settings. Museums experimenting with face detection across photographic collections, or with visitor analytics, should take advice before proceeding.
- **Anything that influences access to rights or services.** If an AI system were ever used to decide who gets access to a service, a benefit or an opportunity, it could tip into the high-risk tier. That is rare in heritage work, but worth flagging in any new project.

## The penalties are real

Breaching the prohibitions can cost up to 35 million euro or 7 percent of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. Other breaches can reach 15 million euro or 3 percent, and supplying incorrect information up to 7.5 million euro or 1 percent. Public and non-profit institutions are not exempt from scrutiny, even if enforcement is proportionate.

## A practical checklist for collections teams

- Make an inventory of every AI feature in your systems, including the ones embedded in tools you bought rather than built.
- Classify each one by risk tier. Most will be minimal or limited.
- Start AI literacy now: short, role-appropriate training for the staff who use these tools.
- Decide how you will label AI-generated descriptions, translations and enhanced images, and build that into your cataloguing workflow.
- Disclose any public-facing AI assistant clearly.
- Keep a record of what each AI process did to a record: which model, when, with what confidence, and whether a human reviewed it.

## Where we stand

That last point, provenance, is the heart of it. Long before the law required transparency, good stewardship demanded that we be able to say how a record came to look the way it does. At The Archive and Heritage Group we built Heratio on that principle: AI is a tool that assists the archivist, never one that replaces the human judgement behind the catalogue. AI-assisted work is traceable, human review is part of the loop, and compliance is handled as pluggable, per-market modules rather than baked-in assumptions, because an institution in Lisbon, Lusaka or Toronto faces different rules and deserves a platform that respects that.

The EU AI Act is not a reason to slow down the responsible use of AI in cultural heritage. It is a reason to do it transparently, to document it well, and to keep a person in the loop. The sector has always cared about provenance. The law is finally catching up to us.
