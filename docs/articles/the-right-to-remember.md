# The Right to Remember

### Why the next great infrastructure project is remembering - and what we have to get right on the way there

On the second of September, 2018, the National Museum of Brazil burned for six hours. Twenty million items. Two hundred years of collecting. The oldest human skeleton in the Americas, indigenous languages whose only recordings were on those shelves, sound archives of peoples who no longer exist. Most of it was never digitised. By morning, a large part of a continent's memory was ash.

It was not an accident of one institution. It was a preview. Palmyra was dynamited. Timbuktu's manuscripts were smuggled out in rice sacks ahead of men who wanted them gone. Notre-Dame burned while the world watched. Floods take parish registers, funding cuts close reading rooms, hard drives rot, formats die, and time does the quiet rest. Every archive, museum and library on Earth is fighting the same war, against the same enemy: forgetting.

We have fought that war for centuries with vaults - cold rooms, acid-free boxes, controlled light. And for the last few decades we have fought it with scanners, convinced that if we could only make a digital copy, the memory would be safe.

But here is the uncomfortable truth at the centre of my work: **digitising is not remembering.**

## The problem was never storage

A scan is a photograph of a thing. Memory is the web of meaning around the thing - who made it, for whom, where it came from, what it meant, who it was taken from, what it stood beside, what happened next. That web is exactly what our systems are worst at keeping.

Walk into the average heritage organisation and you find the memory scattered across silos that cannot speak to each other. The photograph lives in one database. The letter that explains the photograph lives in another archive, in another country, catalogued under a different name. The museum object's maker is a record in a third system; the family it was taken from is a footnote in a fourth, if anywhere. The record survives. The meaning fragments. And a fragmented memory is, functionally, a forgetting in slow motion.

This is the real crisis, and it is invisible because nothing is obviously on fire. We have spent a generation producing millions of orphaned files - pixels without context, context without pixels - and called it preservation.

## How I came to think this way

I did not start with a manifesto. I started, like everyone in this field, with the unglamorous basics. Catalogue the collection properly, to international standards - the same rigour whether the thing is a painting, a book, a deed, or a clay pot. Make it discoverable. Then give it a body: a three-dimensional digital twin of the gallery you can actually walk through, with a layer that watches the real room's light and humidity, a model that forecasts decay before it happens, and a guide that can speak.

Each step taught the same lesson, and it was never the lesson I expected. The technology was never the point. A better catalogue is not the goal. A prettier 3D room is not the goal. They are scaffolding. The thing being built behind all of it is **memory that lasts and means something** - and once you see that, you cannot unsee how far the whole sector still is from it, and how close the tools have quietly come to making it possible.

We are at a hinge moment. For the first time, the pieces exist - machine reading of handwriting, models that can extract people and places from text, knowledge graphs that link entities across collections, immersive interfaces, cheap 3D capture, automatic translation. Individually they are features. Assembled with intent, they are something else: the beginnings of an infrastructure for human memory.

## What that infrastructure should be

If we are going to build it - and I believe our generation is the one that has to - it has to hold six qualities at once. None is optional.

**It must be intelligent.** Archivists are drowning in backlog while sitting on a goldmine of undescribed material. The machine should do the first pass: read the handwriting, draft the description, propose the arrangement, extract the names and places and dates - so that human expertise is spent on judgement and care, not on transcription. For the public, intelligence means a guide that answers a real question in plain language, in the room, in the visitor's own tongue, and walks them to the answer. Access stops being a search box and becomes a conversation.

**It must be trustworthy.** We are entering an era where any image, voice or document can be convincingly faked. That makes the authentic primary source more precious, not less - and it puts archives in an extraordinary position. If every digitised source carries a verifiable chain of custody - this is the original capture, here is every transformation, here is what an algorithm touched and what it did not - then the archive becomes society's fact-anchor. The place you go when "is this actually real?" stops being rhetorical. In the age of synthetic everything, provenance is the product.

**It must be connected.** The single most valuable thing we could build is not a better silo. It is the end of silos: one graph of human memory, where following a single person, place, or idea pulls together everything related to it across every institution that chooses to share. Standards like Records in Contexts and CIDOC-CRM already give us the grammar for this - records, agents, activities, places, all in their relationships. What is missing is the will to connect, and a protocol open enough that any institution on Earth can plug in without surrendering control. Think less "platform" and more "the TCP/IP of cultural memory."

**It must be just.** A connected, honest provenance graph forces a reckoning that the field has too long deferred. So much of what sits in the great collections of the world was displaced, bought under duress, or simply taken. A memory infrastructure that can trace an object's journey can also trace it home - and where physical return is slow or contested, it can at least let heritage live with its community of origin in digital form, on the community's terms, in the community's voice. This is the part that cannot be automated and must not be imposed. It has to be built with people, not for them. Done wrong, it is extraction with better tooling. Done right, it is a quiet act of repair.

**It must be alive.** A memory that only sits in a vault is half-dead. The next leap is heritage you can interact with: a body of work you can question and that answers from its own sources; endangered languages relearned from the recordings and texts in the archive; lost places - demolished districts, razed monuments, vanished streetscapes - reconstructed from the evidence and walked again. Not nostalgia. Resurrection grounded in records.

**It must be open.** None of this matters if it is locked behind geography, wealth, or ability. The measure of success is simple: a child in a township and a researcher in a capital and a grandmother tracing her family can all reach the same memory, on whatever device they have, in their own language, for free. Memory that is not open is not memory. It is property.

## The questions we cannot get wrong

I am wary of techno-optimism that skips the hard parts, so let me name them plainly, because the discussion I actually want is here.

Who governs a shared memory of humanity? A single company or country must not own it; concentration of memory is concentration of power, and history is unkind to those who held that power. It has to be federated, open, and accountable to the communities whose memory it holds.

How do we let AI help without letting it lie? A model that drafts a catalogue record can also hallucinate one. The answer is not to refuse the help but to make every machine contribution labelled, sourced, and reversible - provenance applied to the tools themselves, not only the artefacts.

Who decides what is remembered, and how? Description is never neutral. The categories we use, the names we privilege, the stories we foreground - these are acts of power. An infrastructure of memory must make space for contested histories and multiple voices, not flatten them into one official record.

And who pays for forever? Preservation is a permanent obligation funded by impermanent budgets. We have not solved the economics of remembering at civilisational scale, and pretending otherwise is how archives quietly die.

## The end goal

Strip away the systems and the standards and the roadmaps, and what is left is a single sentence I am willing to be measured against:

**That humanity never forgets.**

One living, trusted memory of all human culture - every people, every place, every story - captured before it is lost, woven into a single graph, provable as true, and open to everyone on Earth, forever. Not a vault of dead records. A memory that speaks: that you can walk through, question, and hear in any culture's own voice.

That is not a product. It is a responsibility - and it is bigger than any one of us, which is exactly why it is worth giving a career to. The institutions that hold the world's memory have always understood that they are keeping something in trust for people not yet born. The tools have finally caught up to the ambition. What we lack is not capability. It is the decision to treat remembering as the infrastructure it has quietly always been - as essential as roads, as worthy as clean water, as urgent as anything we build.

The National Museum of Brazil should never have been a single point of failure for a continent's memory. Nothing should be. The whole point of building this is so that the next time something burns - and something always burns - we do not lose the memory. We only lose the building.

So I will put the question to the people who do this work, and to anyone who has ever been moved by a thing kept safe by someone they never met:

**What should the future of cultural memory look like - and what is the one thing we cannot afford to get wrong on the way there?**
