## The collection: Helmets over Time

"Helmets over Time" traces the development of the combat helmet across four
civilisations and roughly three thousand years - from the royal war-crown of New
Kingdom Egypt, through the iron galea of the Roman legions and the flat-topped great
helm of the Crusading knight, to the fluted turban helmet of the Ottoman cavalry.

The through-line is a single design problem solved four different ways: how to protect
the head without sacrificing the vision, hearing, breathing and mobility a fighter
needs. Read in sequence the objects show three long-running trade-offs being
re-balanced - coverage versus the senses, protection versus weight, and the helmet as
armour versus the helmet as a statement of rank, faith or identity. They also track the
shift in materials from cloth and leather, to bronze and iron, to worked steel, and the
parallel rise of the helmet as a surface for decoration and inscription. Each object is
presented as an interactive 3D model, with uncompressed source assets preserved
alongside the access models.

## Egyptian War-Crown Helmet (Khepresh tradition)

A stylised ancient-Egyptian war helmet in the tradition of the khepresh, the "blue
crown" or war crown worn by New Kingdom pharaohs (roughly 1550-1070 BCE) in battle and
in ceremony. The khepresh is shown in reliefs and statuary on kings such as Thutmose,
Akhenaten, Tutankhamun and Ramesses, often when driving a chariot into battle, and it
came to signify the warrior-king.

Important interpretive note for visitors: the khepresh is known almost entirely from
depictions. No physical example survives archaeologically, and true metal helmets are
not attested for the rank and file of the Egyptian army, who fought in caps of cloth or
leather. Scholars still debate what the crown was actually made of - proposals include
stiffened leather or cloth covered in small discs or sequins, which would explain its
characteristic dotted texture in art. This object is therefore a modern artistic
interpretation of the form rather than a record of a surviving object, and that gap
between rich imagery and absent artefact is itself part of its interest.

Significance: the earliest piece in the sequence and the clearest case of headgear as
royal symbol first and protection second.

## Roman Legionary Helmet (Galea, Imperial Gallic type)

A Roman legionary helmet of the Imperial Gallic type, the standard galea of the early
Imperial heavy infantry (roughly the 1st century CE). The Imperial Gallic pattern grew
out of Gaulish iron-working traditions and was produced in large numbers as Rome
standardised the equipment of the legions.

The type is distinguished by a rounded one-piece iron bowl, a wide flaring neck guard
that protected against downward blows to the nape, embossed "eyebrow" ornament on the
brow, a forehead reinforcing peak against overhead cuts, and large hinged cheekpieces
that shielded the face while leaving the eyes, ears and mouth clear for sight, hearing
and command. Brass trim and decorative bosses were common. The galea is a high point of
practical helmet design in the ancient world: heavy coverage achieved without blinding
or deafening the wearer, and made to be mass-produced and repaired in the field.

Significance: the engineering benchmark of the collection - the senses-versus-coverage
balance solved with hinged, modular components.

## Crusader Great Helm (Pot Helm)

A great helm of the type worn by Christian knights during the Crusades and across
13th-century Europe. In its classic form the great helm is a flat-topped steel cylinder
that fully encloses the head, pierced by narrow vision slits (the "sights") and small
ventilation holes (the "breaths"). It was not worn alone: under it a knight wore a mail
coif and an iron skull-cap (the cervelliere or, later, the bascinet), so the great helm
is the outer layer of a deliberately layered defence.

The great helm trades almost everything for protection. Full enclosure gave
unprecedented coverage against the lance and sword of mounted combat, at the cost of
restricted vision, muffled hearing, poor ventilation and considerable weight bearing on
the head and shoulders - which is why knights often removed it after the initial charge.
Its blank, faceless front also became a canvas for heraldry and crest, turning the
helmet into a bearer of identity on a battlefield where the man inside was otherwise
unrecognisable.

Significance: the protection-maximising extreme of the sequence, and the clearest link
between armour and heraldic identity.

## Ottoman Turban Helmet (Chichak)

An Ottoman chichak, the combat helmet of the sipahi heavy cavalry (in wide use from the
15th-16th centuries and influential across Persia, India and eastern Europe). The
chichak has a rounded, often fluted bowl forged from a single plate and tapering to a
finial. Its defining features are a fixed peak carrying a sliding nasal bar that could
be dropped to guard the face and raised for vision, hinged ear-flaps, and a flared
laminated neck guard - a combination that returns to the Roman priority of protecting
the head while keeping the senses free, but with far greater mobility for a horseman.

Fine examples are richly decorated with gold koftgari (inlay) and with Quranic
inscriptions and prayers invoking divine protection, so the chichak carries faith and
status on its surface much as the great helm carried heraldry. Its sliding-nasal-plus-
ear-flap layout was widely copied and shaped later European cavalry helmets.

Significance: the synthesis piece - mobility, the senses, protection and decoration
brought back into balance, and the helmet again used to express belief and rank.

## Cross-object connections (how the docent can relate them)

- Materials timeline: cloth/leather (khepresh) -> bronze and iron (galea) -> worked
  steel (great helm, chichak).
- The senses-versus-coverage trade-off: the galea and the chichak both keep eyes, ears
  and mouth working; the great helm sacrifices them for total enclosure; the khepresh is
  symbolic rather than protective.
- Helmet as identity: heraldry on the great helm and Quranic koftgari on the chichak are
  two cultures using the helmet's surface to declare allegiance, rank or faith.
- Survival and evidence: the khepresh survives only in images (no artefact), whereas the
  galea, great helm and chichak survive as physical types - a useful talking point about
  how we know what we know.
- Cavalry lineage: the chichak's sliding nasal and ear-flaps influenced later European
  cavalry helmets, linking the Ottoman and European traditions.
